2.4 Navigable

November 7th, 2016

Definition

“Provide ways to help users navigate, find content, and determine where they are.” - World Wide Web Consortium.

What's that mean?

To ensure a more accessible, user -friendly experience, a website should guide users through the content and allow them to bypass things such as the navigation, while also being able to determine where they are on the website.

Why?

Often overlooked, the navigation of a website is very important for every user. If you think about it, when you go to a website and travel through multiple pages on that site, do you look at the navigation bar every single time? Probably not. And when you land on a page on a website, you are more than likely scanning the headlines to find the information you are looking for. This guideline ensures that is the case for every user, to allow everyone to determine where they are on the website, determine where a link will take them, and bypass repetitive information.

Types

The following must be incorporated in your website to pass Level AA of this criterion:

  • Bypass Blocks
  • Page titled
  • Focus order
  • Link purpose
  • Multiple ways
  • Headings and labels
  • Focus visible

Example

I could give so many great examples for this criterion, but I believe the one that is most often overlooked now is the link purpose. In short, this means providing context within the link to where the link goes. Remember all of the links that just say “Click here”? Those would not pass this criterion. “Click here” provides no context to what the link is. Instead, something like “Ryan Schroeder’s Personal Portfolio Website” would provide a lot more information within the link to where the link goes. This is especially important for users who tab through links on a website. If they land on “Click here” they won’t have the surrounding context that might be provided to understand where the link will take them.
